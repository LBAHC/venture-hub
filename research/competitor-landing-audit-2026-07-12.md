# Competitor Conversion-Pattern Audit — Insurance Agency Growth Tools
*Live-site research, July 12, 2026. All headlines quoted verbatim from live pages. Method note: pages were fetched as rendered HTML/text, not screenshotted on a device, so mobile behavior is inferred from structure (nav architecture, CTA repetition) rather than observed — flagged accordingly.*

---

## 1. Agency Revolution (agencyrevolution.com) — Fuse platform

**Above the fold**
- Headline: **"The Only Marketing Platform Your Insurance Agency Needs"**
- Subhead: *"Agency Revolution combines marketing automation, world-class websites, and powerful marketing tools to help you grow your business."*
- Hero visual: decorative vector illustration / checkered geometric patterns — no product screenshot, no photography
- Primary CTA: **"30-Min Consult"** (nav + hero + mid-page + footer); secondary hero CTA "Learn More About Our Solutions"

**CTA strategy:** Call-based, no trial. The "30-Min Consult" framing softens "book a demo" slightly, but it's still a sales conversation as the only conversion path. 4+ CTA variants on the page ("Learn More About Our Solutions," "See What Our Platform Can Do," "Download Now" lead-magnet) — moderately diffuse. Friction: medium-high (talk to a human, no self-serve).

**Social proof:** Heavy. Named testimonials with agency names (Jim King, Waverly Insurance: "Our client retention numbers are well above average at 94%…"), a stats band (99.9% customer satisfaction, 3,000+ active clients, 20% average revenue growth, 10 hours saved weekly), 5-star Google rating callout, 12+ AMS/integration partner logos.

**Pricing:** Fully hidden — no pricing page, no link, nothing.

**Design character:** The most "designed" of the five — cheerful yellow/blue/green palette, playful geometric patterns, bold sans headlines, lots of whitespace. Friendly-agency-brand feel rather than raw SaaS, but still recognizably a marketing-platform template underneath.

**Mobile (inferred):** Deep multi-level nav (5 dropdowns) will collapse to hamburger; "30-Min Consult" is the persistent nav button so it likely survives collapse.

**Does well:** Quantified outcomes ("20% average revenue growth," "10 hours saved weekly") sit close to the testimonials — proof and promise are attached.
**Exploitable weakness:** The headline sells a *platform* ("The Only Marketing Platform… Needs") — it literally leads with the word our positioning attacks. No pricing, no fast entry point; everything funnels to a consult with unknown cost on the other side.

## 2. Levitate (levitate.ai — confirmed current domain)

**Above the fold**
- Headline: **"Relationship marketing as authentic as you are"**
- Subhead: *"Our Happiness Platform combines authentic communication tools, dedicated success specialists and AI to help you build lasting relationships."*
- Hero visual: customer review screenshots + an AI prompt interface, with "Lev the Lion" mascot illustration
- Primary CTA: **"Book a Demo"** (nav + hero + bottom); secondary hero CTA "Check your Online Presence" (an interactive audit tool — clever low-friction hook)

**CTA strategy:** Demo-first, no trial. The "Check your Online Presence" tool is the one genuine friction-reducer on the page — gives value before asking for a meeting. ~3 CTA types. Friction: medium.

**Social proof:** Testimonial-led rather than logo-led — 8+ named customer quotes ("Levitate has paid for itself through Google reviews alone." — Jeffrey Allen Howard). Review badges (G2, Capterra Shortlist) and award logos (Inc. 5000, SaaS Award) pushed down to the footer. Notably **no customer company logos** — only integration logos (Salesforce, Zapier, Gmail, Outlook).

**Pricing:** "Plans" in nav, but the plans page (Essential / Preferred / Prestige tiers) shows **zero dollar figures** — every tier gates to "Book a Demo" / "Talk to Sales."

**Design character:** Soft blue/teal, hand-drawn illustration of "success specialists," friendly mascot throughout. Deliberately warm and human — the most differentiated *tone* of the five, though it's the polished-friendly-SaaS genre (Mailchimp-adjacent) rather than truly distinctive.

**Mobile (inferred):** 10-industry dropdown nav collapses to hamburger; "Book a Demo" is the styled nav button and likely persists.

**Does well:** Sells humans ("dedicated success specialists") alongside software — the closest anyone in the category comes to our angle — and pairs it with a warm, non-corporate visual identity.
**Exploitable weakness:** It's a horizontal platform serving 10+ industries (insurance is one dropdown item among dental, legal, chiropractic…), the headline is vibe with no outcome or dollar figure in it, and "success specialists" still coach *you* to do the work. Pricing tiers exist but every price is hidden — the "Plans" page is a bait-and-gate.

## 3. Better Agency → **now GloveBox CRM** (betteragency.io 301-redirects to glovebox.io/gloveboxcrm/)

⚠️ **Flag:** betteragency.io no longer exists as a standalone site — it permanently redirects to GloveBox's CRM page. The only Better Agency remnant is a "my.betteragency.io" login link in a dropdown. Analysis below is the live GloveBox CRM page, which is the successor product page.

**Above the fold**
- Headline: **"The Insurance CRM That Helps You Sell More Insurance"** (rendered with a line break in the source)
- Subhead: *"Includes 100+ automation campaigns for sales, service, and renewals that help you sell more policies and retain more customers."*
- Hero visual: before/after split image — cluttered spreadsheet vs. clean GloveBox interface, arrow pointing to the "after"
- Primary CTA: **"Schedule Agency Demo"** (nav + hero + repeated mid-page), all routing to a `/discovery-call/` page

**CTA strategy:** Single-minded demo funnel — one CTA repeated relentlessly, framed as "In 30 minutes, see what your agency would look like with GloveBoxCRM." Secondary "VIEW DEMO" (watch, not book). Friction: medium; focus is the best of the five.

**Social proof:** Outcome-heavy and specific: "Since using GloveBox, we've tripled the number of households each CSR manages" (Erb & Young); "My agency is over $25M in premium, and each of my CSRs can handle 2X the amount" (Grant Weber, TruSummit). Stats: 30% service cost reduction, 3X CSR productivity, 40,000+ service transactions in 6 months. 5 agency logos. Proof is scattered mid-page after each feature block. Real owner headshots.

**Pricing:** "Pricing" in nav, but the pricing page shows no numbers — gates to "Schedule Agency Demo."

**Design character:** Direct-response energy inside a modern SaaS shell — bold headlines, before/after imagery, emoji in subheads, GIF product demos. Feels more like a marketer built it than a brand team; punchy but slightly scrappy/template.

**Mobile (inferred):** Simpler nav than the others; single repeated CTA means the conversion path survives small screens well.

**Does well:** The before/after hero visual makes the pain concrete in one glance, and every testimonial carries a hard number.
**Exploitable weakness:** Brand chaos — prospects who knew Better Agency land on a differently-named product with zero explanation, which burns trust. And "100+ automation campaigns" is exactly the setup-burden promise that exhausted owners have stopped believing: someone still has to configure and run 100 campaigns.

## 4. AgencyZoom (agencyzoom.com)

**Above the fold**
- Headline: **"You sell the policy. We automate the rest!"**
- Subhead: three value pillars — sell faster, boost retention, analyze growth
- Hero visual: product dashboard screenshots
- Primary CTAs: **"START FOR FREE"** + **"BOOK A DEMO"** paired in nav, hero, and footer

**CTA strategy:** The only one of the five with genuine self-serve — 14-day free trial alongside demo booking (Calendly). Two-CTA pair repeated consistently, low decision noise. Friction: lowest in the category.

**Social proof:** "Join over 35,000+ users boosting their book of business"; agency logos (The Horton Group, SteelKey, The SIG Insurance Agencies, KC Insurance Group, Insfinity); testimonial from Mark Bailey, Bailey Family Insurance: "I can't live without this tool now. My agency has not only grown, we're thriving." All placed mid-to-lower page.

**Pricing:** **Most transparent of the five.** Pricing in nav, and the page shows real numbers: Essential $149/mo, Growth $199/mo, Pro $349/mo (up to 7 users; Allstate-specific plans $99–129/mo; 20% annual discount; 14-day trial).

**Design character:** Clean, competent, forgettable B2B SaaS — dashboard screenshots, standard hierarchy, all-caps buttons. Safe corporate; could be any vertical SaaS with the logo swapped.

**Mobile (inferred):** Standard hamburger collapse; paired CTAs likely reduce to buttons at hero and footer.

**Does well:** Lowest-friction funnel in the category — real prices plus a real free trial plus segmentation by agency type (Independent, Allstate, Farmers, State Farm…) that makes the pitch feel targeted.
**Exploitable weakness:** The headline promise "We automate the rest!" is a lie of omission our positioning is built to expose — AgencyZoom automates nothing by itself; the owner still has to build pipelines, write sequences, and watch dashboards. Pure tool, zero human service, generic look.

## 5. InsuredMine (insuredmine.com)

**Above the fold**
- Headline: **"AI-Powered Revenue Intelligence for Insurance Agencies"**
- Subhead: *"The Growth Engine Behind High-Performing Agencies"*
- Hero visual: multiple dashboard/interface mockups in a horizontal band
- Primary CTAs: **"TRY FOR $1"** (nav) + "Explore AI Features" / "Request a Demo" (hero)

**CTA strategy:** Hybrid — a $1/14-day paid trial (interesting micro-commitment mechanic, directly relevant to our $500 sprint) plus demo requests plus "Contact Sales." 4-5 different CTA texts; somewhat cluttered. Friction: low-medium.

**Social proof:** Volume play — 18+ client agency logos, 20+ integration logos (HawkSoft, AMS360, Applied Epic, NowCerts…), 8 named testimonials ("The pipeline has been revolutionary for Great Harbor… it makes things incredibly efficient." — Clay Wadsworth), "$8 Billion in Premiums" managed stat, SOC 2 Type II badge, industry-association memberships.

**Pricing:** Pricing page in nav but it's a shell — literal "$.00" placeholders and "One Price, No Surprise" copy with **no actual price**, gated behind "Book a Demo." Worst-faith pricing page of the five.

**Facepalm-level nav:** Features menu lists 14 items (Sales Pipeline, Opportunities, Goal Management, Task Management, Account 360, Drip Campaign, Automation…). This *is* the overwhelm our audience feels.

**Design character:** Blue/teal enterprise-SaaS: gradient accents, dashboard mockups, icon grids. Dense, feature-catalog feel. 2026 buzzword veneer ("AI-Powered Revenue Intelligence") on a classic feature-dump page.

**Mobile (inferred):** Enormous mega-menus will be painful collapsed; "TRY FOR $1" as the nav button is probably the persistent mobile CTA.

**Does well:** The "$1 trial" is a smart friction-cutter, and the proof stack (SOC 2, $8B premiums, dense logos) is genuinely reassuring for skeptics.
**Exploitable weakness:** "Revenue Intelligence" + a 14-item feature menu = maximal tool-fatigue trigger. A page claiming "One Price, No Surprise" over a "$.00" placeholder is self-parody — pricing dishonesty we can contrast against directly.

# SYNTHESIS

## What the category has converged on
1. **Headline = platform identity claim**, not customer outcome: "The Only Marketing Platform…", "The Insurance CRM That…", "AI-Powered Revenue Intelligence…". Four of five lead with what the *software is*; none leads with a dollar outcome for the reader.
2. **Demo as the default conversion event.** 4 of 5 route primarily to a scheduled sales call; only AgencyZoom offers true self-serve. The demo is universally dressed up ("30-Min Consult," "discovery call," "see what your agency would look like").
3. **Pricing theater.** 3 of 5 have a "Pricing"/"Plans" nav link that leads to a page with no prices (InsuredMine's shows "$.00"). Only AgencyZoom publishes numbers. The category trains visitors to expect a bait-and-gate.
4. **Product-screenshot or illustration heroes.** Dashboards (AgencyZoom, InsuredMine), before/after UI (GloveBox), or brand illustration (Agency Revolution, Levitate). Nobody shows a human doing work.
5. **Mid-page social proof stack**: agency-name testimonials with retention/productivity percentages, logo walls, a big cumulative stat (3,000+ clients / 35,000+ users / $8B premiums), badges in the footer. Proof is abundant but generic-shaped — every site has the same stack in the same order.
6. **Feature-catalog information architecture**: mega-menus with 5-14 product items, "Solutions/Features/Resources/Pricing" nav — the anatomy of software being sold to be *operated by the buyer*.
7. **Design register:** blue/teal, bold geometric sans, gradient accents, dashboard mockups, icon-grid feature sections, whitespace. Levitate softens it with a mascot and hand-drawn art; the rest are interchangeable. The category *look* is "competent SaaS template."

## Category blind spots our positioning exploits
- **Every page implicitly assigns the buyer homework.** "100+ automation campaigns," 14-feature menus, template libraries, certifications — all of it says *you will configure and run this*. Not one site says "we will do it for you." The gap shows up structurally: no site has a "how the engagement works" section, no named human who does the work, no week-by-week timeline — because there's no service to describe. Our page can be built around exactly those elements.
- **Nobody reports in dollars.** Proof is percentages and user counts. "Dollars recovered" as the unit of value is unclaimed language and an unclaimed *visual* (a plain-English recovery report as the hero image would look like nothing else in the category).
- **Nobody names a price above the fold.** A visible "$500. Flat. 14 days." is a pattern interrupt against three bait-and-gate pricing pages the visitor has probably already seen.
- **Nobody agrees with the customer's tool fatigue.** All five must defend software because they sell it. We can open by *validating* the objection every one of them dodges — the exact sentence "You don't need another tool" is a direct hit on Agency Revolution's "The Only Marketing Platform Your Insurance Agency Needs" and AgencyZoom's "We automate the rest!"
- **Nobody looks like a human wrote to a human.** Dashboards and mascots everywhere; no letter, no plain page, no operator's face.

## Recommendations for our landing page

1. **Above-fold structure — anti-platform hero.** Headline is the positioning line ("You don't need another tool. You need someone to run the one you have."). Subhead names the mechanism and unit of value in one sentence: we run your AMS/CRM's quote follow-up, renewals, and cross-sell, and report in dollars recovered. **No dashboard screenshot.** Hero visual should be either (a) a mock one-page dollars-recovered "Recovery Report" summary, or (b) nothing — a typographic hero. Any UI screenshot instantly re-files us as "another tool."

2. **CTA pair, ranked not equal.** Primary button: **"Start the $500 Sprint"** (or "Book the 14-Day Sprint — $500 flat"), price *in the button* — that alone differentiates from five demo-gated competitors. Secondary, visually quieter (text link or ghost button): "Or book a 15-min fit call first." Keep this exact pair everywhere; never introduce a third CTA verb (the diffuse-CTA sites — Agency Revolution, InsuredMine — are the cautionary tale; GloveBox's single relentless CTA is the model for discipline).

3. **Put the retainer price on the page too.** "$500 sprint → $1,000–1,500/mo if you keep us" stated plainly, mid-page. After Levitate's price-free "Plans" and InsuredMine's "$.00," published pricing reads as integrity and pre-qualifies the call. AgencyZoom proves the category's buyers tolerate — and reward — visible pricing.

4. **Replace the logo wall with a "how it works" week-by-week.** We have zero social proof; competitors have walls of it. Don't fake the stack — substitute *specificity*: Days 1-2 we get access and pull your aged quote list; Days 3-10 we run the sequences from your own system; Day 14 you get a dollars-recovered report. A concrete operational timeline is the proof-of-competence a service business gives when it has no testimonials yet. Optionally add a risk-reversal line ("If the sprint doesn't find recoverable revenue, don't hire us for the retainer") — that does the job a review badge does.

5. **Handle zero proof honestly with a founder block.** Real name, real face, 2-3 sentences on why an operator (not a vendor) is doing this, signed. The category shows customers' headshots but never the seller's. One honest human beats an empty testimonial slider; never use placeholder logos or "trusted by agencies nationwide" fluff — this audience has seen every version of it.

6. **Mobile-first because the click is from email on a phone.** One screen = headline + one primary button. Sticky bottom bar with "Start the $500 Sprint" after first scroll. No nav menu at all if possible (a single-purpose page needs no hamburger — every competitor drags a mega-menu around). Sections must work as single-column cards; the week-by-week timeline should read as a vertical checklist.

7. **Design character: depart from "SaaS blue" toward "trusted operator letter."** The category look to reject: blue/teal gradients, geometric bold sans, dashboard mockups, icon grids, mascots. Our departure: editorial/document aesthetic — warm paper-white or cream background, a serif or serif-led pairing for headlines (reads as letter/report, not app), generous line length, black-ink text, one restrained accent (e.g., ledger green or signature blue used only for CTAs and dollar figures), no icons, no gradients. It should feel closer to a well-set memo from a competent operator than a product site — because that *is* the product.

8. **Weaponize their own words in an objection section.** A short block: "Already paying for AgencyZoom, Levitate, or InsuredMine? Good. Keep it. That's the tool we'll run." Naming the tools (a) proves stack fluency, (b) reframes competitors as inputs rather than alternatives, and (c) captures searchers who arrive with those brands in mind.
